# <font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);">The genotype–phenotype relationship and evolutionary genetics in the light of the Metabolic Control Analysis</font>
[https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0303264723001752](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0303264723001752)

## <font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);">Abstract</font>
[Metabolic control analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/metabolic-control-analysis)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> has long been used as a systemic model of the genotype–phenotype (GP) relationship. By considering </font>[kinetic parameters](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/mathematics/kinetic-parameter)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> and </font>[enzyme](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzyme)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> concentrations as reflecting the genotype level and </font>[metabolic fluxes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/metabolic-flux)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> or pools as phenotypes related to fitness, MCA has given a biological basis to the relationship between these two levels. The non-linear and concave relationship between enzymes and fluxes can account for common </font>[genetic effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/genetic-determinism)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> that reductionist approaches have been powerless to explain, such as the dominance of active alleles over less active alleles, the various types of epistasis and </font>[heterosis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/agricultural-and-biological-sciences/heterosis)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);">, and reveals the structural links between these genetic effects. The </font>[summation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/mathematics/summation)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> property of the flux control coefficients accounts for the L-shaped distribution of </font>[Quantitative Trait Locus](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/agricultural-and-biological-sciences/quantitative-trait-loci)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> (QTL) effects, irrespective of other possible causes. Metabolic models of response to selection results in evolutionary scenarios that are markedly different from those derived from the classical infinitesimal model of quantitative </font>[genetics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/genetics)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);">. In particular, evolution towards selective neutrality appears to be a consequence of the diminishing return of the flux-enzyme relationship. In this paper, we survey the historical and recent achievements of MCA in genetics, </font>[quantitative genetics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/agricultural-and-biological-sciences/quantitative-genetics)<font style="color:rgb(31, 31, 31);"> and evolution, focusing on epistasis and the evolution of flux in relation to enzyme concentrations.</font>

# <font style="color:rgb(32, 32, 32);">Evolutionary Plasticity and Innovations in Complex Metabolic Reaction Networks</font>
[https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1000613](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1000613)

<font style="color:rgb(32, 32, 32);">Genome-scale metabolic networks are highly robust to the elimination of enzyme-coding genes. Their structure can evolve rapidly through mutations that eliminate such genes and through horizontal gene transfer that adds new enzyme-coding genes. Using flux balance analysis we study a vast space of metabolic network genotypes and their relationship to metabolic phenotypes, the ability to sustain life in an environment defined by an available spectrum of carbon sources. Two such networks typically differ in most of their reactions and have few essential reactions in common. Our observations suggest that the robustness of the </font>_<font style="color:rgb(32, 32, 32);">Escherichia coli</font>_<font style="color:rgb(32, 32, 32);"> metabolic network to mutations is typical of networks with the same phenotype. We also demonstrate that networks with the same phenotype form large sets that can be traversed through single mutations, and that single mutations of different genotypes with the same phenotype can yield very different novel phenotypes. This means that the evolutionary plasticity and robustness of metabolic networks facilitates the evolution of new metabolic abilities. Our approach has broad implications for the evolution of metabolic networks, for our understanding of mutational robustness, for the design of antimetabolic drugs, and for metabolic engineering.</font>

# <font style="color:rgb(11, 11, 11);">Refining a key metabolic innovation in </font>_<font style="color:rgb(11, 11, 11);">Escherichia coli</font>_
[https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111)

<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">The origins of evolutionary novelty are notoriously difficult to predict in advance or even to pinpoint in retrospect. Qualitatively new traits can arise due to complex mutational events, such as regulatory changes (</font>[1](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r1)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">), or exaptation of genes coding for existing functions (</font>[2](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r2)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">). This often involves multiple mutations that are all required to produce a novel function, making evolutionary innovation historically contingent on the chance order of random mutational events (</font>[3](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r3)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">). Lenski and colleagues recently discovered a striking example of this process in their long-term evolution experiment (LTEE) in </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">Escherichia coli</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">: after more than 31,000 generations of evolution, 1 of 12 replicate lines </font><font style="color:#DF2A3F;">evolved the ability to metabolize citrate under oxic conditions</font><font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> (</font>[4](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r4)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">). Inability to grow aerobically on citrate is often used to define </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">E. coli</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> as a species (</font>[5](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r5)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">), so the spontaneous appearance of this citrate-metabolizing phenotype offers an unprecedented opportunity to study the mechanisms underlying a major evolutionary innovation. The work by Quandt et al. (</font>[6](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r6)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">) in PNAS sheds new light on the genetic basis of this novel trait.</font>

<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">With this picture in mind, Quandt et al. (</font>[<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">6</font>](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r6)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">) set out to identify the potentiating and refinement mutations. This task is complicated by the need to distinguish these mutations among more than 70 other mutations present in early Cit++ clones. To solve this problem, Quandt et al. introduce a technique, recursive genomewide recombination and sequencing (REGRES), to map the mutations involved in the Cit++ phenotype. They first backcross an evolved Cit++ clone (previously isolated from generation 33,000 of the LTEE) (</font>[<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">7</font>](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r7)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">) with its Cit– ancestor. This generates a pool of recombinant strains, each with some subset of the mutations present in the evolved clone. The authors screen these recombinant strains to isolate several that have the Cit++ phenotype and repeat the backcrossing procedure several times. They then sequence the resulting backcrossed Cit++ clones to identify a minimal set of mutations required to confer the Cit++ phenotype (</font>[<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">Fig. 1A</font>](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#fig01)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">).</font>

<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">This minimal set of mutations turned out to be surprisingly small. As expected, Quandt et al. found the actualizing </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">rnk-citG</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> mutation, along with a known refinement mutation, </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">dsbG–insA-9</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">. The only other mutation on the list was </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">dctA*</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">, a mutation in the promoter region of </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">dctA</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> that appeared in the LTEE after the </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">rnk-citG</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> mutation. No potentiating mutations were found. To exclude the possibility that some mutations were missed at the sequencing stage, Quandt et al. (</font>[<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">6</font>](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1323189111#core-r6)<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">) show that the combination of just two mutations, </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">rnk-citG</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> and </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">dctA*</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">, is indeed necessary and sufficient to convert the ancestral Cit– strain into a Cit++ strain. This contradicts the all-or-none epistasis hypothesis and suggests a different scenario for the evolution of the Cit++ phenotype. Even though potentiating mutation(s) were required for the actualizing </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">rnk-citG</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> mutation to become beneficial and were therefore essential to the original evolution of the citrate metabolism, they are rendered obsolete by the subsequent </font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);">dctA*</font>_<font style="color:rgb(38, 38, 38);"> refinement mutation.</font>
