## Highlights
+ [Enzymes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/lysozyme) are inherently selective owing to their precise catalytic machinery.
+ Discriminating against similar substrates is, however, an explicitly evolved trait.
+ Accuracy comes with cost; discrimination at substrate level is most costly.
+ Evolutionary optimization may involve tradeoffs between enzyme accuracy and rate.

I discuss some <font style="color:#DF2A3F;">physico-chemical</font> and<font style="color:#DF2A3F;"> evolutionary aspects</font> of enzyme accuracy (selectivity, specificity) and speed (turnover rate, processivity). Accuracy can be a beneficial side-product of active-sites being refined to proficiently convert a given substrate into one product. However, exclusion of undesirable, non-cognate substrates is also an explicitly evolved trait that may come with a cost. I define two schematic mechanisms. _Ground-state discrimination_ applies to enzymes where selectivity is achieved primarily at the level of substrate binding. Exemplified by DNA methyltransferases and the ribosome, ground-state discrimination imposes strong accuracy-rate tradeoffs. Alternatively, _transition-state discrimination_, applies to relatively small substrates where substrate binding and chemistry are efficiently coupled, and evokes weaker tradeoffs. Overall, the mechanistic, structural and evolutionary basis of enzymatic accuracy-rate tradeoffs merits deeper understanding.

## Introduction
Living systems exhibit accuracy at all levels. We are, however, also aware of the limits of accuracy, through a diversity of phenomena ranging from [genetic mutations](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/gene-mutation) and translational errors to [enzyme promiscuity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzyme-promiscuity) and metabolite damage [[1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0005)]. Limited accuracy, noise, or messiness as defined by some, is also of crucial importance, as it provides the <font style="color:#DF2A3F;">starting points for evolutionary innovations</font> [[2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0010)]. Take for example the existence of coincidental protein conformations and promiscuous [enzymatic activities](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzyme-activity). Although never selected for, these latent, promiscuous [conformers](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/conformational-isomer) and activities serve as starting points if and when new enzymatic functions are needed [[3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0015)]. The prevalence of inaccuracy, its evolutionary benefits but also its inevitable cost, prompt the question of why? Why is the accuracy of biological systems, and [enzymes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/lysozyme) in particular, limited?

## The evolutionary origins of inaccuracy
The evolutionary benefits of infidelity raise the tempting, albeit largely baseless, hypothesis that inaccuracy, and other phenomena related to life's complexity, are explicitly promoted by natural selection [[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0020)]. An alternative view is that accuracy comes with a significant cost, and is thus being maintained at an acceptable rather than maximal level [[1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0005), [3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0015)]. 

Let us consider these two alternative hypotheses in more detail.

+ (i) _<font style="color:#DF2A3F;">Inaccuracy is a selected trait</font>_: Imagine an organism with a perfect replication and [DNA](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/deoxyribonucleic-acid) repair machinery. Most mutations are deleterious, and indeed, the [mutation rates](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/mutation-rate) in nearly all organisms are extremely low. In _Escherichia coli_, it takes, on average, about 106 generations for a gene to be hit by any one mutation. Even a mild increase in mutation rate is deleterious under optimal growth conditions, and following prolonged growth, also under challenging environments [[5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0025)]. Mutations, however, are absolutely essential, as they provide the basis for adaptation. So are replication fidelity and repair explicitly kept from being too accurate? Namely, are life's molecular systems shaped by evolution with a dual purpose — performing their current function with maximal efficiency, and maintaining the capacity for innovation (evolvability [[6](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0030)])?

Despite its popularity, this hypothesis is rarely applicable, let alone unambiguously supported by data. As a rule, selection very rarely acts to limit the accuracy of replication, or of DNA repair enzymes, or in fact of any other [enzyme](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/lysozyme). Most claims for noise or infidelity being explicitly shaped by evolution comprise Panglossian reasoning — if it is there, it must be for a good reason [[1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0005), [2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0010), [4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0020)]. For example, the linkage between stress and higher mutational rates in bacteria is largely indirect [[7](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0035)]. One rare exception is [RNA](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/rna) viruses where increasing replication fidelity impairs fitness [[8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0040)]. The high error rate of [Polio's](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/poliomyelitis) RNA polymerase is under direct selection, and is tightly kept at approx. two mutations per genome per replication [[8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0040)] (a rate that is _a priori_ >104-fold higher than in non-viral organisms on a per base [pair basis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/pair-base) [[9](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0045)]).

+ (ii) _Inaccuracy — the lesser evil_: The alternative to (i) above, and in my view the default explanation for noise and inaccuracy is that, at a population level, their deleterious effects are too weak to be eliminated by selection. Thus, as a default, biological molecules are as inaccurate as they can afford to be, and not as accurate as they could, or need to be, particularly so because accuracy comes with a cost. Proof-reading, or editing is an evident hallmark of cost: [Hydrolysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzymatic-hydrolysis) of non-cognate aminoacyl-tRNAs by aminoacyl-tRNA [synthases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/synthase) (AARSs), or the [nuclease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/nuclease) action of [polymerases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/polymerase), impose a direct cost of [ATP](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/atp) molecules for remaking these bonds (for a general model of speed-accuracy tradeoffs in systems with proof-editing see [[10](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0050)]).

Here, I discuss mechanisms of discrimination that occur within the primary active-site and do not involve undoing of enzymatic steps as in proof-editing. As discussed here, a potential cost of enzymatic accuracy is reduced rate, or lower [processivity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/processivity). Specifically, enzyme rates tradeoff with accuracy and with a range of other demands, thus resulting in a fine balance between the benefit and the cost of accuracy. Indeed, the [catalytic efficiency](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/catalytic-efficiency) (_k__cat__/K__M_) of the “average” enzyme is ∼105 M−1 s−1 [[11](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0055)], orders-of-magnitude lower than the [diffusion rate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/diffusivity) limited ‘perfect enzyme’ [[12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0060)]. Our understanding of the evolutionary constrains that shape enzymes is therefore relevant to our understanding of what is a ‘perfect enzyme’.

## The evolutionary origins of accuracy
Two alternatives can be noted — the first ascribes specificity to chance, and the second to a necessity:

+ (i)

_Accuracy is an inherent side-product of catalytic proficiency_: Evolution shapes the active-sites of enzymes to maximize specific interactions between active-site residues and the reaction's transition-state (_positive selection_). Active-sites are unlikely to materialize the same interactions with substrates and transition-states that differ from the cognate one, certainly not to the same degree. Thus, selectivity can be an inherent side-product of [catalytic efficiency](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/catalytic-efficiency). [DNA](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/deoxyribonucleic-acid) polymerases may indeed represent such a case whereby catalytic efficiency for incorporation of then cognate base correlates with fidelity [[13](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0065)].

+ (ii)

_Accuracy as an explicitly evolved trait_: In many enzymes, the inherent selectivity might be physiologically insufficient, especially when substrates similar to the cognate one are present at competing concentrations. In these cases, selectivity is likely to be the outcome of an explicit selection against certain substrates (_negative selection_).

How relevant is negative selection in explicitly shaping the specificity of natural enzymes? Laboratory evolution experiments suggest that positive selection on its own is insufficient to induce high selectivity [[14](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0070)]. As suggested by (i) above, selection toward one substrate and transition-state reduces the accommodation of other substrates. However, it seems that large losses in the original activity only come at the late stages of optimization toward a new activity [[14](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0070)]. Further, at this advanced stage, the improvement in the new activity per mutation is very small (diminishing returns). This means that inherent selectivity emerges slowly. High selectivity may inherently appear only when the newly evolving activity reached high levels [[15](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0075)], or may not occur at all if the selection pressure for high catalytic efficiency is weak [[14](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0070)]. Indeed, numerous natural enzymes show broad, or multi-specificity, and thus catalyze multiple transformations within the same cell [[14](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0070), [16](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0080)]. Conversely, laboratory evolution experiments indicate that negative selection — selection against alternative substrates, induces selectivity much more effectively [[17](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0085), [18](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0090)], and sometimes via mutations that abolish binding of the undesirable ligand with no effect on the evolving ligand [[19](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0095)]. Thus, in highly specific enzymes, selectivity is likely to be an explicitly evolved trait — that is, the combined outcome of _positive selection_ for the cognate substrate and of _negative selection_ against the nocognate ones.

## Accuracy-rate tradeoffs
Accuracy-rate tradeoffs in enzymes relate to what degree increased accuracy leads to lower catalytic efficiency. [Enzyme reactions](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzyme-mechanism) being second-order, rates are usually expressed in the _k__cat__/K__M_ value for the cognate substrate. Accuracy is typically expressed as the _A value_, the ratio of _k__cat__/K__M_ values for the cognate substrate (the correct, physiological substrate) and alternative, non-cognate one(s) ([Box 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#tb0010)). The magnitude of tradeoff cannot be derived from the [kinetic parameters](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/kinetic-parameter) of a single enzyme variant or reaction. Rather, a comparison is needed, either of the wild-type enzyme to a mutant that exhibits higher or lower accuracy (as exemplified with DNA methyltransferaes below; see also Ref. [[20](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0100)]), or of the same enzyme under different reaction conditions, as discussed below with the ribosome's magnesium regulated rate-accuracy.

Box 1

Defining enzyme accuracy

The accuracy or selectivity of an enzyme is generally defined as the ratio of _k__cat_/_K__M_ values for the cognate substrate (i.e. the correct, physiological substrate) and the non-cognate one (i.e. promiscuously bound and reacted substrates), or the _A value_.(1)A=(kcat/KM)cog(kcat/KM)non

Under steady-state, initial rates conditions the expected molar ratio of the desired product versus the non-desired one would be proportional to the ratio of _k__cat__/K__M_ values and substrate concentrations. This relationship holds regardless if the latter are below or above the _K__M_ [[21](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0105), [34](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0170)]:(2)[P]cog[P]non=A*[S]cog[S]nonwhereby _A_ is the discrimination factor, [S] is substrate concentration, and [P] is product concentration. Assuming a simplified scenario of _K__M_ ≈ _k_−1_/k_1, whereby _k_1 and _k_−1 correspond to the rate constants of substrate-enzyme association, and dissociation, respectively:(3)A=(kcatcog/kcatnon)⋅(k1cog⋅k−1non)k−1cog⋅k1non

Thus, accuracy can be driven by: (i) faster association of the cognate substrate relative to the noncognate one (_k_1_cog_ > _k_1_non_); and/or (ii) faster dissociation of the non-cognate substrate (_k_−1_non_ > _k_−1_cog_); and/or (iii) faster conversion of the enzyme-substrate complex into the transition-state, and consequently to product (_k__cat__cog_ > _k__cat__non_).

In some cases, for example, DNA polymerases, or the ribosome, fidelity is defined as the sum of catalytic efficiencies for all potential substrates (cognate as well as noncognate nucleotides, or tRNAs) divided by the catalytic efficiency for the noncognate substrate in question; [Σ(_k__cat__/K__M_)]/(_k__cat__/K__M_)_non_. Assuming, however, that (_k__cat_/_K__M_)_cog_ ≫ (_k__cat__/K__M_)_non_, this expression roughly equals to _A_ (e.g. [[39](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0195), [47](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0235)]).

Measured as discussed above, the accuracy-rate tradeoff is a [macroscopic property](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/molar-property) that does not relate to the molecular mechanism by which accuracy is achieved in a particular enzyme and to why it affects rate. Regarding the mechanism, two modes of discrimination can be schematically described: substrate, or _ground-state discrimination_, and catalytic or _transition-state discrimination_ ([Figure 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#fig0005)).

Theoretically, ground-state discrimination should be manifested in differences in _K__M_ values, and transition-state discrimination in _k__cat_. However, enzymatic microscopic rate constants are coupled, and thus both _K__M_ and _k__cat_ may be affected.

## Ground-state discrimination
Higher rate of association, and/or lower dissociation rate, of the cognate relative to the noncognate enzyme–substrate complexes drive ground-state discrimination (_k_1, _k_−1; [Box 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#tb0010)). The rate-accuracy tradeoff that accompanies ground-state discrimination is therefore inherent. The [activation energy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/reaction-activation-energy) barrier of an [enzymatic reaction](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/enzymatic-reaction) corresponds to the [free energy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/gibbs-free-energy) difference between the enzyme-substrate and the enzyme-transition-state complexes. A lower free energy of the enzyme–substrate complex thereby means a lower _k__cat_ [[21](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0105)]. Rate constants, be they _k__cat_, _k_1, or _k_−1, relate to free energy in the same manner (Δ_G_° = −_RT_ ln(_k_)). And thus, as [substrate concentrations](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/substrate-concentration) approach, let alone exceed the _K__M_ value, the tradeoff becomes maximal — any improvement in selectivity derived from tighter binding of the cognate substrate reduces _k__cat_ by the same factor. Tighter binding therefore results in a parallel decrease in _K__M__cog_ and _k__cat__cog_, and thus the discrimination factor that relates to _k__cat_/_K__M_ remains the same (Equation 3; [Box 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#tb0010)).

Ground-state discrimination is not only saddled by strong accuracy-rate tradeoffs, but also has an inherently limited potential for high discrimination. When the _K__M_ value is close to, or lower than substrate concentration, the discrimination factor has limited optimization potential as described above. The _K__M_ value may evolve to be higher than the physiological substrate concentration, but that means lower ratio of cognate to noncognate _K__M_ values, and thus a lower discrimination factor ([Box 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#tb0010)). Finally, the discrimination afforded by substrate binding is also limited by the interactions that mediate substrate binding being inherently weak.

A notable derivation from the above analysis is that selectivity is therefore achieved most effectively by exclusion of noncognate substrates (repulsive interactions) not by tighter binding of the cognate substrate. This conclusion also relates to the above-discussed role of negative selection in shaping selectivity, and to discrimination against larger noncognate substrates being inherently higher than that for smaller ones (discussed below).

## How far can molecular distinction go?
Discrimination is driven by positive interactions (hydrogen bonds, electrostatics, Van der Waals, etc.) as well as negative ones (steric and electrostatic clashes). The latter are far more pronounced, as indicated by the acute asymmetry of potential bond energy functions (e.g. Morse potentials). Consequently, discriminating against substrates smaller than the cognate one is particularly challenging — the loss of Van der Waals contacts following the removal of one [methyl group](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/methyl-group) corresponds to <10 kJ/mol of binding energy, that translates to <100-fold difference in _k__cat_/_K__M_. Additionally, the voids associated with binding of smaller substrates are often taken over by water molecules. Further, the small energy differences associated with binding noncognate substrates cannot be enhanced by conformational changes (e.g. induced fit; see also [[22](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0110), [23](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0115)]), or any other ‘trick’ that is not energy consuming (i.e. proof editing).

Consider, for example, the discrimination between [tyrosine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/tyrosine) and [phenylalanine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/phenylalanine). The APhe/Tyr exhibited by phenylalanine ammonia-lyase is ∼104. In contrast, tyrosine ammonia-lyase exhibits an ATyr/Phe value of ∼10 [[24](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0120)]. The same trend is observed in aromatic [amino acids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/amino-acids)[hydroxylases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/oxygenase) [[25](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0125)]. The ease of discriminating against larger substrates is also utilized in the proof-editing of amino acids-tRNA syntheases such as ThrRS. The proof-editing site selectively recognizes and [hydrolyzes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/enzymatic-hydrolysis) tRNAs misacylated with the noncognate [serine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/serine); whereas the additional methyl group of the cognate Thr-tRNAThr, the undesirable substrate for proof-editing, is readily excluded by steric clashes [[26](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0130)]. A notable case of selectivity against a smaller substrate with no proof-editing is tyrosyl-tRNA-synthase, as discussed in section ‘Transition-state discrimination’.

Proteins can distinguish differences that are even smaller than a methyl or hydroxyl group. For example, by virtue of a unique hydrogen bond, bacterial [phosphate binding proteins](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/phosphate-binding-protein) discriminate against arsenate (identical valency and p_K_a values as phosphate and only 4% larger in size) by factors of up to 5000 [[27](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0135)]. However, the phosphate-utilizing enzymes tested thus far show almost no discrimination [[28](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0140)]. Indeed, enzymes seem to utilize phosphate primarily by [electrostatic interactions](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/electrostatic-interaction), and phosphate and arsenate have the same anionic structure [[28](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0140), [29](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0145)]. It also remains unclear, however, to what degree discrimination trades off with transport rate, that is, that poorer discrimination would afford higher rates of [phosphate transport](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/phosphate-transport). Phosphate-binding proteins have only been tested for binding of phosphate and of competing anions in isolation. Transport, however, occurs via an [ABC transporter](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/abc-transporter) to which the phosphate cargo is handed over. Transport measurements may reveal to what extent high discrimination against arsenate affects rates. In most likelihood, it does.

## Ground-state discrimination — DNA methyltransferases
Bacterial DNA methyltransferases that mediate site-specific genome [methylation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/methylation) recognize specific double-stranded-DNA sequences and transfer a [methyl group](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/methyl-group) from the [coenzyme](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/coenzyme) S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) to the target base. Coenzyme binding and methyl transfer occur within a Rossmann-fold domain (the catalytic domain) whereas binding of the DNA target sequence is usually mediated by a separate domain, the target recognition domain. _K__M_ values for cognate [DNA sequences](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/dna-sequence) are typically in the sub-μM range.

In accordance with the hypothesis of accuracy-rate tradeoffs, relaxing the target specificity results in a dramatic increase in catalytic efficiency. For example, the bacterial DNA methyltransferase M.HaeIII targets GG<u>C</u>C sequences, and methylates the internal [cytosine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/cytosine) (underlined). This enzyme was subjected to directed evolution toward the methylation of AGCC — a ‘star’ site differing from the cognate one by one base [[30](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0150)], and later for a range of other new targets [[31](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0155)]. The outcome was relaxed specificity, or ‘generalist’ enzymes — namely enzyme variants capable of methylating the original target (GGCC), the noncognate target selected for (e.g. AGCC, or GGACC), as well as other targets that were not selected for (e.g. CGCC, or GCGC) [[31](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0155)]. The catalytic efficiency of the evolved variants increased relatively to wild-type enzyme, not only for the targets selected for (as expected) but also for the original target. Indeed, the latter was unexpected and remained unexplained in the original publication. For example, M.HaeIII variants evolved toward AGCC exhibited aprox. 700-fold increase in _k__cat__/K__M_ value for AGCC (the selection target) and about 10-fold increase for methylating GGCC, the original target [[30](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0150)].

The changes in M.HaeIII's specificity were mediated by mutations that disabled a ‘gate keeper’ residue, Arg225, within the target recognition domain [[30](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0150)] to either Gly or Ser [[31](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0155)]. The side-chain of Arg225 interacts with the first [guanine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/guanine) of the target sequence (G1; [Figure 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#fig0010)). This interaction primarily mediates ground-state selectivity, as Arg225 is located >20 Å from where methyl transfer occurs. This interaction's strength is also unlikely to vary significantly upon formation of the transition-state complex, and M.HaeIII is accordingly constrained by a strong specificity-rate tradeoff.

## Rate-accuracy tradeoffs — the ribosome case
There exists a strikingly tight correlation between the catalytic efficiency of ribosome-catalyzed [peptidyl transfer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/peptidyltransferase) and its accuracy. Accuracy and rate were systematically measured for Lys-tRNALys (UUU as anti-codon) with its cognate codon (AAA) and with a series of non-cognate codons ranging from the alternative lysine codon, AAG, to codons that differ in the first and second bases and encode other [amino acids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/amino-acids). The varying parameter was a physiologically relevant one — [magnesium ion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/magnesium-ion) concentration. The higher the magnesium concentration, the higher is the ribosome's catalytic efficiency and the lower is its accuracy [[34](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0170)].

The magnitude of the accuracy-rate tradeoff varies. Mismatches in the second base that lead to drastically different amino acids showed the strongest tradeoff. Thus, preventing the more potentially deleterious translational errors comes with a higher cost in rate. In the most extreme case, tRNALys-ACA (threonine codon), increasing rate (_k__cat_/_K__M_) with the cognate tRNA by 50% was accompanied by nearly sevenfold reduction in the discrimination factor. For AAG, the alternative lysine codon, accuracy was reduced by only 2.4-fold. Interestingly, a similar tradeoff is seen upon varying temperature. The rate of translation in mouse cells increases _ca._ 1.5-fold between 32 and 37 °C. The rate of mistranslation of various non-lysine codons to give a lysine containing protein increases by 1.5 up to 5-fold, with the highest increase being the mistranslation of a [stop codon](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/stop-codon) into lysine [[35](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0175)].

The cellular magnesium concentration in _E. coli_ varies within the range applied in these experiments (1.5–7.5 mM). Given that the average frequency of translational errors is 10−3–10−4, the lowest fidelity range (high magnesium) would result in _ca._ one amino acid mutation in one out of three [protein molecules](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/protein) (for average size of about 300 amino acids). Although about 40% of protein mutations seem to have a measurable deleterious effect [[36](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0180), [37](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0185)], this may be an affordable price at high growth rates, where cells divide fast and their protein content is rapidly renewed. At no or slow growth, when survival is a determining factor, this frequency of translational errors is likely to be costly (see also [[38](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0190)]).

The ribosome also fits the ground-state discrimination model. Binding of the tRNA's anti-codon to the mRNA and peptidyl transfer are spatially separated. Additionally, peptidyl transfer is accompanied by a ∼50 Å translocation of the mRNA–tRNA complex. A remote recognition site and high configurational plasticity result in tight mRNA–tRNA recognition being highly costly in terms of turnover rate.

## Transition-state discrimination
Discrimination at the transition-state level is potentially far more effective than ground-state discrimination. The binding of the cognate and noncognate substrates may occur with similar affinity. However, non-cognate substrates bind such that transition-state stabilization is not fully realized ([Figure 1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#fig0005)). Higher selectivity could be achievable by this mode, as the [free energies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/gibbs-free-energy) associated with transition-state complexes are much higher than those of ground-state complexes. Indeed, computational simulations revealed the power of this mode in discriminating noncognate bases in DNA polymerases. Upon binding the cognate base, the active-site dipoles are optimally reorganized to maximize transition-state stabilization. However, upon binding non-cognate (wobble) bases, the enzyme relaxes into a state whereby transition-state stabilization is significantly compromised [[23](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0115)]. Accordingly, DNA polymerases exhibit high accuracy (even without proof-editing) as well as high rates (e.g. PolI exhibits _k__cat_ > 100 s−1 with misincorporation frequency in the absence of proof-editing being <10−3 [[39](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0195)]). As indicated by the thio-effect, the chemical step is more rate-limiting for the noncognate base than for the cognate one, supporting a mode of transition-state discrimination. Accordingly, in oppose to cases of rate-accuracy tradeoffs described above, in DNA polymerases, catalytic efficiency positively correlates with fidelity [[13](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0065)]. This correlation, however, is not observed between mutants of the same [polymerase](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/polymerase), but rather between different polymerases and, it particularly relates to DNA repair polymerases that evolved to have low fidelity as well as low rates [[13](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0065)]. A DNA polymerase was also found to exhibit no trade-off upon temperature changes — although the _k__cat_ of [Geobacillus](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/geobacillus) PolI increased with temperature (∼7-fold, between 22 and 50 °C) accuracy remained unchanged [[39](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0195)]. This in contrast to the ribosome where increased rate resulted in significantly decreased accuracy [[35](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0175)].

Another notable case of selectivity is tyrosyl-tRNA-synthase (TyrRS) that activates [tyrosine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/tyrosine) with 105-fold higher efficiency than [phenylalanine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/phenylalanine) [[40](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0200)] and with no proof-editing [[41](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0205)]. Although TyrRS seems to be acutely shaped by selection against phenylalanine, certain mutations increase the discrimination factor. However, the increased discrimination factor comes partly at the expense of reduced _k__cat__/K__M_ for tyrosine [acylation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/acylation) [[42](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0210)]. Discrimination is foremost manifested in _K__M_ (_K__M_Tyr = 2.2 μM, _K__M_Phe = 62 mM, measured by [pyrophosphate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/pyrophosphate) exchange); the _k__cat_ values differ only by a factor of ∼4. However, since substrate binding is coupled to turnover, _K__M_ is a kinetic measure (unlike _K__S_). Thus, differences in _K__M_ values do not necessarily rule out transition state discrimination. Discrimination at the _k__cat_ level is observed, however, with d-tyrosine. The _K__M_ value for d-tyrosine is in fact lower than for l-tyrosine (fourfold, for [Bacillus](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/biochemistry-genetics-and-molecular-biology/bacilli) TyrRS) whereas the _k__cat_ values are up to 83-fold higher [[43](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0215)].

_To conclude_, evolutionary tradeoffs shape the adaptive landscape, enforcing an efficient compromise between multiple parameters [[44](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0220), [45](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0225)]. Specifically, the evolutionary optimization of enzymes regards multiple properties some of which can be contradictory. Stability-activity tradeoffs, for example, have been widely addressed [[46](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0230)]. However, apart from proof-editing, it is rarely realized that enzyme selectivity can have a cost in catalytic rate. Outlined here are several hypotheses with respect to rate-accuracy tradeoffs that may be the subject of future explorations (summarized in [Box 2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#tb0015)).

Box 2

Key hypotheses and standing questions

+ (i)

Tradeoffs between accuracy and rate might be yet another constrain under which enzymes evolve. However, beyond proof-editing, the generality of this tradeoff, and its molecular basis, remain largely unexplored. Foremost, methods for relevant measurements of rate-accuracy tradeoffs need to be develop. Few approaches might be applicable, although each enzyme class may demand its own solution:

    - •

Perturbing the concentration of reaction components such as [magnesium ion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/magnesium-ion). The latter affects many enzymes, especially with phosphorylated substrates, for example, the ribosome [[34](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0170)].

    - •

Varying the reaction's temperature at the range well under the enzyme's melting temperature (e.g. Refs. [[35](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0175), [39](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0195)]). Note, however, that the interpretation of temperature-rate dependencies may not be simple [[46](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0230)].

    - •

Comparison of the wild-type enzyme to mutant(s) selected for higher rates with noncognate substrates. Enzyme variants selected from repertoires of randomly incorporated mutations are preferable, as rationally designed mutations tend to exhibit much stringer tradeoffs [[30](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0150), [31](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0155)].

+ (ii)

The mechanistic and evolutionary modes by which an enzyme's selectivity is achieved are key determinants of the magnitude of its rate-accuracy tradeoffs.

+ (iii)

The selectivity potential of transition-state discrimination is higher, and the accuracy-rate tradeoff is predicted to be weaker than for ground-state discrimination, or rate and accuracy may even positively correlate [[13](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0065), [23](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1367593114000635?via%3Dihub#bib0115)]. However, transition-state discrimination might be applicable only to relatively small and rigid substrates, whereby substrate binding and [chemistry](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/chemistry/chemistry) are mediated by overlapping active-site residues. Further, direct experimental evidence for the model of ground-state _versus_ transition-state discrimination is obviously needed.

+ (iv)

Selectivity is most effectively achieved (i.e. higher discrimination factors, and minimal cost in rate) by exclusion of noncognate substrates rather than by tighter binding of the cognate substrate; namely by repulsive rather than attractive interactions.

+ (v)

Accordingly, _negative selection_ against undesirable substrates is likely to be a key force in shaping enzyme selectivity, also because of the minimal tradeoff. Measuring the rate-accuracy tradeoff along parallel trajectories that optimize the same enzyme for either rate, or for both rate and accuracy, would therefore be of interest, and might validate this hypothesis and the above one (iv).
