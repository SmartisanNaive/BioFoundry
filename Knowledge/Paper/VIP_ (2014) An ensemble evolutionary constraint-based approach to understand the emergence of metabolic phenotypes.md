Abstract

Constraint-based modeling is largely used in computational studies of metabolism. We propose here a novel approach that aims to identify ensembles of flux distributions that comply with one or more target phenotype(s). The methodology has been tested on a small-scale model of yeast energy metabolism. The target phenotypes describe the differential pattern of ethanol production and O2 consumption observed in “Crabtree-positive” and “Crabtree-negative” yeasts in changing environment (i.e., when the upper limit of glucose uptake is varied). The ensembles were obtained either by selection among sampled flux distributions or by means of a search heuristic (genetic algorithm). The former approach provided indication about the probability to observe a given phenotype, but the resulting ensembles could not be unambiguously partitioned into “Crabtree-positive” and “Crabtree-negative” clusters. On the contrary well-separated clusters were obtained with the latter method. The cluster analysis further allowed identification of distinct groups within each target phenotype. The method may thus prove useful in characterizing the design principles underlying metabolic plasticity arising from evolving physio-pathological or developmental constraints.

## 1 Introduction
Cellular functions emerge from complex, often non-linear interactions among many different molecular components (Alberghina and Westerhoff [2005](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR2)). These complex, system-level properties are best studied by systems biology approaches that integrate molecular analysis (often genome-wide) with mathematical modeling and simulation. Metabolism has been an area of intense application of modeling efforts. A metabolic network is defined by a set of compounds (nodes) and a set of reactions that connect them (edges). Since they consider a relatively small number of metabolites and reactions, small scale (core) metabolic reconstructions are usually modeled in a mechanistic, kinetic way. These core models often deal with a single metabolic pathway, such as glycolysis (Teusink et al. [2000](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR37)), mitochondrial bioenergetics (Chang et al. [2011](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR7)) or the tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) (Oliveira et al. [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR23)), although models integrating different pathways exist (e.g. Cortassa et al. [2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR9); Wu et al. [2007](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR39)).

Information from fully sequenced annotated genomes, the rapid availability of “omics” data in databases, and the development of a wealth of computational tools in systems biology have allowed a growing number of cell-specific reconstructions of metabolic networks on a genome-wide scale. Mechanistic, kinetic modeling is currently not applicable to genome-scale reconstructions. Therefore, computational investigation of genome-scale metabolic networks makes use of a constraint-based modeling approach (Gianchandani et al. [2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR14); Terzer et al. [2009](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR36)) that exploits the knowledge about the structure of cell metabolism, while disregarding dynamic intracellular behavior on the basis of a pseudo-steady state assumptions for internal metabolites. This assumption is supported by the observation that intracellular dynamics are so much faster than environmental changes that they can be assumed to reach a steady state instantaneously.

The starting point of constraint-based modeling is the stoichiometric information embedded in the metabolic network, which can be represented with a stoichiometric matrix where the rows stand for the metabolites involved in each reaction, whereas the columns correspond to the reactions themselves. The null space of this matrix mathematically represents the mass balance for each intracellular metabolite and expresses all flux distributions, or phenotypes, that can be achieved by a given metabolic network. Additional constraints, such as irreversibility or capacity constraints, can be incorporated (Covert et al. [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR10)). Thus, the imposition of constraints leads to a space of feasible flux (i.e., the amount of each metabolite transformed per unit of time) distributions, each flux distribution representing a functional state. Assuming that cell behavior is optimal with respect to an objective, optimization methods, such as flux balance analysis (FBA), can be used to calculate a single “optimal” flux distribution, that allows to achieve a specific objective. To this end, a single physiologically plausible function is defined and maximized. Moving from the postulate that organisms have developed control structures to ensure optimal growth in a constraining environment, different objective functions (briefly OFs) have been used by different authors and for different purposes, even in the same organism. As it has been recently pointed out, an appropriate OF is of paramount importance in FBA studies (Feist and Palsson [2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR13)).

Not only may we be limited in our knowledge of what the true OF is, but even if we precisely knew the OF that drives evolution, we still could not exclude that the organism is found in a sub-optimal space. In this regard, some recent work addressed the question whether cells operate optimally or not: in Schuetz et al. ([2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR32)) authors showed, following multi-objective optimization theory, that metabolism works near the Pareto-optimal surface of a space defined by competing objectives; while in Harcombe et al. ([2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR16)) it is described how the biomass maximization is a good, but not perfect, measure of flux change during laboratory evolution.

If we generalize the approach to interacting populations, i.e. different tissues in a multicellular organism or interacting (competing) microbial populations, or to physio-pathological developmental switches, then the definition of a plausible objective function is even less straightforward (Mardinoglu et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR20)), and maximization of cellular biomass cannot be taken as the unique descriptor of the cell functional state. Constraint-based predictive modeling could therefore benefit from new strategies, not limited to the identification of a single “optimal” flux distribution for a given OF. In this regard, Monte Carlo sampling is emerging as an approach for unbiased analysis of the solutions space aimed at describing global network properties (Schellenberger and Palsson [2009](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR31)).

We are interested in understanding how a cell may develop alternative metabolic behaviors by an appropriate alteration of its metabolic fluxes, i.e. in understanding which collection of flux distributions are compatible with a given metabolic behavior. Therefore, in this work we propose a novel approach that neither focuses on an optimal solution nor analyzes the properties of the entire solution space, but rather focuses on analyzing the generic properties of ensembles of solutions that comply with defined phenotype(s), hence the name _ensemble approach_.

As opposed to the dominant sampling algorithm of choice—the Markov chain Monte Carlo method—in our sampling approach the network structure is maintained fixed, while some constraints are systematically varied, thereby simulating changes in environmental or intracellular conditions. The approach may benefit from the adoption of an evolutionary algorithm, hence the name _evolutionary approach_.

We tested our algorithm using a core model of yeast energy metabolism. The target phenotypes describe—according to Boolean or algebraic formulation—salient input-output features of “Crabtree-positive” and “Crabtree-negative” yeasts. The results of the procedure indicate which flux distributions are compatible with a given metabolic phenotype and help in understanding how a cell may develop alternative metabolic behaviors by an appropriate alteration of its metabolic fluxes.

## 2 Ensemble-evolutionary FBA (eeFBA)
The principle behind constraint-based modeling is the progressive application of constraints in order to exclude the flux distributions that cannot be achieved by a metabolic network. The first flux distributions to be excluded are those that do not satisfy the quasi-steady state assumption and thus the equation \(d {\mathbf {x}}/dt = S \cdot {\mathbf {v}} = 0\), where \(d{\mathbf {x}}/dt\) are time derivatives of metabolite concentrations represented by the product of the \(m \times n\) stoichiometric matrix _S_ multiplied by the vector of fluxes \({\mathbf {v}} = ( v_1 v_2 \dots v_n )\), where \(v_i\) is the flux of reaction _i_, _n_ is the number of reactions, and _m_ is the number of metabolites. The obtained null space corresponds to the so called _solution space_\(\Sigma \). In addition, it is possible to exploit the information on the boundaries of each flux \(I_i = [v_i^{min}, v_i^{max}] \) to define the space of acceptable fluxes \(I = I_1 \times I_2 \times \dots I_n\). The combination of these two informations, defines the _feasible solutions space_\(\Phi = I \cap \Sigma \). Finally, FBA aims at identifying a single optimal flux distribution through optimization of an objective function \({\mathbf {z}} = \sum _{i=1}^{n} c_i v_i\) where \(c_i\) is a weight indicating how much reaction _i_ contributes to the objective function. For a more comprehensive description of FBA the reader is referred to Orth et al. ([2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR24)), Raman and Chandra ([2009](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR29)).

According to the approach to FBA we are proposing here, the objective function \({\mathbf {z}}\) it is not intended to represent a physiologically plausible objective (e.g. maximization of growth) or a bioengineering design goal (e.g maximization of biofuels production), rather it is seen as a means to explore the feasible solutions space. The maximization of a random set of objective functions, in order to obtain corners in the space of allowed solutions has already been proposed before by Bordel et al. in Bordel et al. ([2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR6)).

It has indeed been recently proven that random sampling in the region of feasible flux distributions is an effective way to characterize its content (Schellenberger and Palsson [2009](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR31)). For instance, it has been used to study the statistical distribution of the flux values of all the reactions in a genome-scale model (Bordel et al. [2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR6)) and to define modules of reactions whose fluxes are positively correlated (Almaas et al. [2004](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR3); Papin et al. [2004](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR25)). In this work, instead, we use random sampling in order to seek functional states that agree with experimentally observed phenotypes. The sampling method is therefore combined with a search algorithm in order to find solutions with specified properties within the feasible solutions space, as it will be better elucidated in Sect. [2.1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Sec3).

Hence, the expected phenotype must be formally defined according to biological evidence. To this end, we propose to define it in terms of a metabolic response to environmental conditions variations. For instance, we may want to observe a redistribution of fluxes as a consequence of a variation in nutrient availability that is compliant with experimental observations. In order to identify a possible metabolic response of the system (or phenotype) to an environmental variation, or more precisely variations in the boundaries of a reference flux _i_, i.e. \(v_i \in I_i^l =[v_i^{l,min}, v_i^{l,max}] \) (a different interval for each environmental condition _l_ with \(l= 1, \ldots , L\)), it is convenient to define a _phenotypic feasible solution space_\(\Phi _l = I_i^l \cap \Sigma \) where \(I_l = I_1 \times \dots \times I_i^l \times \dots \times I_n \). The union of the _L_ distinct \(\Phi _l\), one for each environmental condition, defines the _phenotype space_\(\Phi = \bigcup _l \Phi _l\). To realize a metabolic response it is necessary to study the system behavior under the _L_ environmental conditions, that means to assign to the same fixed OF \({\mathbf {z}}_j\) in each of the distinct \(\Phi _l\) a distinct optimal fluxes distribution \(\hat{s}_j^l\) exploiting linear programming. It is then possible to define the set \({\mathcal {S}}_j = \left\{ \hat{s}_j^l \right\} _{l=1,\ldots ,L}\) which is our metabolic response of the system when exposed to the different conditions. By means of the creation of several, _R_, different OFs \({\mathbf {z}}_j\) it is possible to explore \(\Phi \) by sampling a set of metabolic responses \({\mathcal {S}}=\left\{ {\mathcal {S}}_j \right\} _{j=1,\ldots ,R}\). It is worth repeating that the objective function is not intended to have any biological meaning beyond this and it is assumed to represent just a particular _network condition_.

Suppose that we know that in the organism under study an increase in the rate of a nutrient uptake, which can be simulated by increasing the boundaries that constrain the corresponding flux \(v_i\), is accompanied by an increase in another flux \(v_j\) (e.g. the secretion rate of a given metabolite), then we want to identify within the phenotype space those phenotypes that do reflect this behavior. Hereinafter, the term _solution_ will tacitly have this broader meaning: a set of functional states that, besides fulfilling the imposed constraints and their variations, abides by the metabolic response definition (hereinafter also referred to as _metabolic response constraint_).

If the metabolic response definition is sufficiently loose, for example if the behavior in correspondence of intermediate levels of nutrient uptake is not specified (as in the definition of the Crabtree effect (CE) given in Sect. [3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Sec4)), we may find that alternative solutions, characterized by different objective functions and thus different network conditions, satisfy the specified property and therefore come out with an _ensemble_ of solutions. The procedure to obtain such ensemble will be described in the next section.

By analyzing the typical behavior of ensembles of different solutions to the same metabolic response constraint, we can depict the emergent behaviors of our metabolic network and analyze which functional states have the capability to sustain the metabolic response at issue.

It might also be the case that the solutions in the ensemble cluster into different groups, thereby representing different conditions of the network resulting in the same metabolic response. These clusters may be identified by means of a clustering algorithm, as shown in the application example in Sect. [3.2.1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Sec7). There it is also shown how we could use the clustering outcome to select those solutions that are biologically more plausible and therefore refine our ensemble.

At this point, we may also want to compare the ensemble of solutions representative of a given metabolic response (e.g. a physiological response) against an ensemble of solutions representative of a different response (e.g. an altered response) in order to identify sets of fluxes that behave differently in the two cases and may therefore be associated to candidate pathways responsible for the different metabolic response. It is worth mentioning that this comparison can follow different strategies: in Sect. [3.2.2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Sec8) we show an example in which a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test is performed in order to determine the fluxes that differ significantly between the two ensembles.

### 2.1 Sampling and searching methods
_Sampling the solution space:_ Random sampling in the region of feasible solutions has most commonly been performed by means of the “hit-and-run” sampler, according to which a point that is known to lie inside the region of possible solutions is perturbed randomly in order to obtain points that are still inside it. For the above mentioned reasons, in this work, we instead make use of linear programming with a random set of objective functions to be maximized.

In Bordel et al. ([2010](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR6)) random objective functions were generated by selecting random pairs of reactions and assigning them random weights. In order to maximize the variability of the solutions sampled we do not limit to the case in which just a pair of fluxes must be maximized, on the contrary we allow any number of reactions to partake in the random objective function. Any reaction is therefore assigned a weight \(c_i\), which is generated by drawing a random number in (0, 1]. The cardinality of the set of reactions having \(c_i > 0\) represents the number of reactions that concur to the objective. In order to have an uniform probability to obtain an objective involving from one to all reactions, the fraction of \(\tau \) considered reactions is drawn at random as well, with uniform probability in (0, 1].

An instance _j_ of the objective functions \({\mathbf {z}}_j\) is therefore defined as \({\mathbf {z}}_j = \sum _{i = 1}^{n} c_i v_i\), where \(c_i\) takes value 0 with probability \(\tau \) and takes a random value with uniform probability in (0,1] with probability \(1-\tau \).

To every \({\mathbf {z}}_j\) it is then assigned an _optimal solution_\(\hat{s}_j^l \in \Phi _l\) determined by means of the optimization process typical of FBA.

_Populating the ensembles of solutions:_ Bearing in mind that, within our framework, a solution \(\hat{s}_j^l\) is valid if, by moving the boundaries of a given flux \(v_i\) while maintaining \({\mathbf {z}}_j\) constant, the observed redistribution of the “optimal” fluxes respects the metabolic response constraint, to a phenotype of interest \(\alpha \) corresponds an ensemble of matching solutions \({\mathcal {A}} \subseteq {\mathcal {S}}\).

Within this framework, it is possible to convert \(\alpha \) in a Boolean fashion (the metabolic response constraint is either respected or not) to the Boolean expression _A_ and then one can simply randomly sample as many points (i.e. \({\mathbf {z}}_j\)) as possible and filter them accordingly, \({\mathcal {A}} = \{ {\mathcal {S}}_j \ | \ {\mathcal {S}}_j \in {\mathcal {S}},\, A({\mathcal {S}}_j) = 1 \} \). In the following, this procedure will be referred to as _sampling + filtering_.

At the price of reducing the variability within the ensembles, it is possible to drive the sampling towards a closer description of the metabolic dynamics (i.e. we want the behavior to be as close as possible to the metabolic response constraint), exploring the phenotype space by means of an evolutionary algorithm: specifically in our case, a _genetic algorithm_ (GA) is adopted in order to make a set \(P = \{{\mathbf {z}}_j \}\) of _p_ individuals, corresponding to different sampled objective functions, evolve according to a fitness function \(a({\mathcal {S}}_j)\) that quantifies the distance of the corresponding solutions in \({\mathcal {S}}_j\) from the metabolic response constraint.

In carrying out this search, we are driven by two mutually exclusive aims: on the one hand, we want to focus on the solutions that best agree with the reference phenotype, a property well captured by genetic algorithms; while, on the other hand, we want to include as many solutions as possible to reveal the eventual ensemble properties which characterize the phenotype.

This second aim can be achieved exploiting differently initialized populations for different runs of the GA. It is also possible to amplify the variability within the ensemble \(\mathcal {A}\) selecting, for each run _r_, not only the best solution \(\overline{{\mathcal {S}}_{j,r}}\) but a set of solutions “not too far from the best”. We implemented this by choosing all those individuals which are a standard deviation apart from the best solution fitness value, \({\mathcal {A}} = \{{\mathcal {S}}_{j,r} \ | \ {\mathcal {S}}_{j,r} \in \Phi , a({\mathcal {S}}_{j,r}) \le a(\overline{{\mathcal {S}}_{j,r}}) + \sigma _r \} \).

## 3 eeFBA to investigate the Crabtree effect
In order to better illustrate the general procedure, as well as its potential and challenges, we make use of the Crabtree effect (Deken [1966](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR11)). The CE describes the phenomenon whereby some yeasts (e.g. _Saccharomyces cerevisiae_), regardless of the availability of oxygen, produce ethanol via fermentation in the presence of high external glucose concentrations rather than optimizing the production of energy by means of the oxidative phosphorylation—as other yeasts do (also known as Crabtree-negative yeasts, e.g. _Kluyveromyces_)—therefore consuming less oxygen.

In investigating the implications of the two different metabolic responses, for the sake of simplicity, we apply our approach to the very simplified model of yeast metabolism drawn in Fig. [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig2), which takes into account only the main pathways and metabolites involved in the selected phenomenon.

### 3.1 Crabtree-positive and negative phenotypes formalization
The CE can be defined as the ability to repress respiration and oxidative phosphorylation as glucose concentration increases (Diaz-Ruiz et al. [2011](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR12)). It is easily observed experimentally as the persistence of aerobic alcoholic fermentation—with a concomitant reduction in the respiration rate—when a pulse of glucose is added to the culture medium of yeasts (Barford and Hall [1979](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR4); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)) or when yeasts in glucose-limited chemostats are grown at increasingly higher dilution rates, so that the flux of glucose from the medium to the cell progressively increases (Porro et al. [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR28)).

In order to apply the eeFBA to the study of the CE it is necessary to mathematically describe the metabolic response constraints corresponding to Crabtree-positive (C\(\oplus \)) and Crabtree-negative (C\(\ominus \)) phenotypes respectively.

In this regard, we have taken into account the behavior of oxygen uptake \( v_o \) and ethanol secretion \( v_e \) fluxes as they are respectively representative of oxidative phosphorylation and pyruvate metabolism, whose activity traditionally define the CE. Moreover, we consider these two fluxes as a function of the glucose uptake \( v_g^{} \) because it has been observed (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)) a strong difference between C\(\oplus \) and C\(\ominus \) yeasts in the kinetics of glucose uptake. This latter can be described by means of the glucose uptake flux series \(\{ v_g^{i} \ | \ \forall \ i <\,j \quad v_g^{i} < v_g^{j} \}_{i,j=1, \dots , L} \) which is the set of glucose values at which each _in-silico_ experiment is run.

Additionally, it is necessary to impose in the definitions of the two phenotypes that to a null glucose uptake, \( v_g^{1} = 0\), correspond oxygen uptake and ethanol secretion null fluxes, \( v_e ( v_g^{1} ) = v_o ( v_g^{1} ) = 0\), to faithfully reproduce the biological reality. A characteristic trait of the C\(\oplus \) is that the ratio aerobic glycolysis over respiration grows proportionally to the glucose uptake, meaning that, at maximal values of glucose uptake the ethanol secretion flux must overcome the oxygen uptake flux. Moreover, it is necessary to discard those solutions in which respiration is not simultaneously employed with fermentation, more quantitatively speaking, the oxygen consumption and ethanol secretion fluxes must be at least once non null in the interval considered. All the aforementioned constraints can be summarized in the logical expressions of Eq. [1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ1) used to filter the solutions relative to the C\(\oplus . \)

$$\begin{aligned}&\left( \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_e ( v_g^{l} )} > 0 \right) \wedge \left( \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_o ( v_g^{l} )} > 0 \right) \wedge \left( v_e ( v_g^{1} ) = 0 \right) \wedge \left( v_o ( v_g^{1} ) = 0\right) \wedge \nonumber \\&\quad \left( v_e ( v_g^{L} ) - v_o ( v_g^{L} ) > 0\right) \end{aligned}$$

(1) 

To explore a wider variety of emergent behaviors that satisfy Eq. [1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ1) we do not filter for intermediate levels of glucose uptake, but we focus on the relationship between fermentation and respiration at the extremes of the considered interval.

Along similar lines, we do not impose a null or a specific fermentation level in the C\(\ominus \) yeast; on the contrary, we just verify that the oxygen consumption does not grow faster than ethanol production as a function of glucose uptake (i.e. is not C\(\oplus \)), and of course that oxygen uptake must increase as function of glucose uptake (i.e. for maximal values of glucose uptake it has to be greater than zero). The C\(\ominus \) metabolic response Boolean constraint is therefore stated as follows:

$$\begin{aligned} \left( v_e ( v_g^{1} ) = 0 \right) \wedge \left( v_o ( v_g^{1} ) = 0 \right) \wedge \left( v_o ( v_g^{1} ) - v_o ( v_g^{L} ) < 0 \right) \end{aligned}$$

(2) 

To reproduce the two phenotypes of interest, we firstly sampled the space \(\Phi \), we filtered these solutions by means of Expression [1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ1) to populate the C\(\oplus \) ensemble and then we tested the remaining solutions with Expression [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ2) to populate the C\(\ominus \) ensemble.

According to this framework, a solution in which the ratio of aerobic glycolysis over respiration slightly grows as a function of glucose uptake will have the same probability to be selected as a solution in which this rate significantly increases.

If we rather intend to bias the search algorithm in order to preferably select an ensemble of C\(\oplus \) solutions in which the metabolic response constraint is maintained as much as possible (e.g. the increase in the aerobic glycolysis/respiration is maximum), then we should define a fitness function able to do it. For the C\(\oplus \) case, a possible fitness function \(a({\mathcal {S}}_j)\) that when minimized allows to achieve this goal is:

$$\begin{aligned} \left( v_e ( v_g^{1} ) + v_o ( v_g^{1} ) + \frac{ v_o ( v_g^{L} ) }{ v_e ( v_g^{L} ) } \right) \quad \frac{( V_{\text {MAX} } \ L)^2}{ \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_e ( v_g^{l} ) \quad \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_o ( v_g^{l} )}}} \end{aligned}$$

(3) 

In this heuristic expression, and later in Expression [4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ4), the requirement that neither respiration nor fermentation should be observed when glucose uptake is null is described by means of the sum terms \( v_e ( v_g^{1} ) + v_o ( v_g^{1} )\), whereas the ratio \(\frac{ v_o ( v_g^{L} ) }{ v_e ( v_g^{L} ) }\) stands to amplify the fact that the ethanol secretion should overcome the oxygen uptake at the highest glucose intake level. This latter term requires to solve the possible indefinite form that arises when \( v_o ( v_g^{L} ) = v_e ( v_g^{L} ) = 0\); if it is the case, we choose to solve it by setting the fraction value to infinity to penalize those phenotypes with no ethanol production at high glucose. The last term \( \frac{( V_{\text {MAX} } \ L)^2}{ \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_e ( v_g^{l} ) \quad \sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_o ( v_g^{l} )} }} \) is present to favor those solutions that have a not null glucose kinetics of the two main fluxes, with \( V_{\text {MAX} } \) being the higher bound value for all the fluxes so that \(\sum _{l=1}^{L}{ v_o ( v_g^{l} )} \le L V_{\text {MAX} } \).

For the C\(\ominus \) case, a possible fitness function \(a({\mathcal {S}}_j)\) can instead be defined as follows:

$$\begin{aligned} v_e ( v_g^{1} ) + v_o ( v_g^{1} ) + \frac{ v_e ( v_g^{L} ) }{ v_o ( v_g^{L} ) } + \frac{ v_o ( v_g^{1} )}{ v_o ( v_g^{L} )} \end{aligned}$$

(4) 

The fact that in the C\(\ominus \) phenotype the oxygen uptake should overcome the ethanol secretion at high glucose, is described by the third term of Expression [4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Equ4). In this case the choice to solve the corresponding indefinite form has been to impose the fraction value to infinity to penalize those solutions with no oxygen production at high glucose levels. The last term is present to favor those solutions which increase respiration as glucose uptake increases, and according to this, the relative indefinite form has been fixed to infinity if \( v_o ( v_g^{1} ) = v_o ( v_g^{L} ) = 0\).

### 3.2 Results
All the results described in this section have been obtained by means of the sampling + filtering approach or by a genetic algorithm; while it is not necessary to describe the implementation of the Boolean filter, it is worthy to state here the parametrization[Footnote1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fn1) that has been used to tune the GA [see Mitchell ([1996](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR22)) for a general introduction on GA and Patil et al. ([2005](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR27)) for an application to metabolism]:

+ the initial population has been set to 100 individuals;
+ we use tournament selection of size 4;
+ we make use of single point crossover: a random crossover point \(h\) is drawn then for the child vector \(\mathbf {c}\), then \(c_i\) is equal to the \(c_i\) of the first parent for all \(i<h\) or to the \(c_i\) of the second parent otherwise;
+ we apply a uniform random mutation: each entry in the vector \(\mathbf {c}\) can be replaced by another uniform random number in [0,1], with a probability of 0.05.

#### 3.2.1 Crabtree-positive and Crabtree-negative ensembles
When populating the ensembles \({\mathcal {A}}_{\oplus }\), with solutions relative to the C\(\oplus \) phenotype, and the ensemble \({\mathcal {A}}_{\ominus }\), with solutions relative to the C\(\ominus \) phenotype, with the two different strategies, sampling + filtering or GA, significantly different populations are obtained. Before proceeding with the explanation of the analyses that are relevant to characterize the biological model, we briefly discuss the differences in the ensemble populations.

By sampling 42,448 random objective functions, by means of the filtering + sampling algorithm, we have obtained an ensemble of 933 C\(\oplus \) solutions (of which 930 are unique solutions, i.e. at least the value of one flux across the different values of glucose uptake is not identical) and en ensemble of 4,746 C\(\ominus \) solutions (of which 4,471 are unique). This result suggests that when performing unbiased sampling of the phenotype space there is a much higher probability to observe the metabolic response typical of the Crabtree-negative yeasts than of observing the CE (estimated as 11 % against a 2 % respectively). It is worth noting that in the 87 % of cases the metabolic response observed is not compliant with any definition, suggesting that our constraint method is effective in excluding biologically implausible solutions form the phenotype space.

When running the genetic algorithm 1,000 times, 7,455 (of which 397 are unique solutions) individuals have been selected according to the C\(\oplus \) fitness function, against the 11,181 (of which 884 are unique) individuals obtained with C\(\ominus \) fitness function. After checking \({\mathcal {A}}_{\oplus }\) and \({\mathcal {A}}_{\ominus }\) with the Boolean filter to discard the false positives, only the population of \({\mathcal {A}}_{\ominus }\) was reduced to 867 individuals; this result highlights the effectiveness of the chosen heuristic.

The comparison of the properties of the ensembles obtained with the two algorithms has revealed significant differences between them. The analysis has been performed by merging the C\(\oplus \) and C\(\ominus \) solutions obtained with a given algorithm and analyzing the resulting dataset by means of a hierarchical clustering (Johnson [1967](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR17)). The dendrogram (not shown here) resulting from the the sampling + filtering case does not allow us to identify well separated clusters of solutions—not even those corresponding to the sets of C\(\oplus \) and C\(\ominus \) solutions. In fact, there are many branches with just a single leaf to indicate the presence of many outliers; this is not surprising if one thinks of how many different behaviors could fit the Boolean expressions of a given metabolic response constraints.

On the contrary, in the dendrogram obtained with the genetic algorithm (shown in Fig. [3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig3)) a distinction between two major groups can be clearly observed. The main branch on the left corresponds to the set of C\(\oplus \) solutions, whereas the main branch on the right hand corresponds to the set of C\(\ominus \) solutions: it is worth stressing that there are no solutions falling into the cluster representative of the wrong ensemble.

Strikingly, some sub-clusters can also be clearly identified within the two ensembles. In order to identify the main ones, we cut the histogram at the value 2,000 on the Y axis (distance) and we selected those sub-clusters composed of more than 10 individuals: 10 clusters have been obtained. From the analysis of the average behavior of the fluxes representative of respiration and fermentation in each sub-cluster, the existence of distinct behaviors compliant with the same metabolic response definition (either C\(\oplus \) or C\(\ominus \) ) clearly emerges: the typical representatives of the five major sub-clusters are depicted in the plots attached to the dendrogram (Fig. [3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig3)).

For those sub-clusters that are most characterizing of the C\(\oplus \) and C\(\ominus \) phenotypes, the complete flux profile has been analyzed: Fig. [4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig4) shows a heat map for all the fluxes of Crabtree-positive (top) and Crabtree-negative (bottom) solutions. Major fluxes differing between the two are labeled 1, 2 and 3. Overall, these markedly different areas in the heat map confirm that a rearrangement of a subset of fluxes is associated with Crabtree-positive and Crabtree-negative solutions (as better detailed in Sect. [3.2.2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Sec8)). Remarkably, a smaller subset of reactions shows differences within each cluster (grey rectangles), Crabtree-negative showing higher plasticity.

#### 3.2.2 Crabtree-positive versus Crabtree-negative average behavior: differentially expressed pathways
In order to analyze the differences between the C\(\ominus \) e C\(\oplus \) ensembles we limit our analysis to the solutions belonging to the clusters (obtained with the genetic algorithm) that better characterize the C\(\oplus \) and C\(\oplus \) phenotypes respectively. For this reasons, the three clusters having an hybrid behavior similar to the small plot in the middle of Fig. [3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig3) have been discarded from the 10 obtained.

In order to identify fluxes that significantly distinguish the two ensembles we used a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test. The statistical test has been applied for all the 10 non-null levels of glucose: the fluxes that are significantly different for at least 9 out of 10 levels of glucose are marked in Fig. [2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig2). In particular red arrows indicate that for a certain reaction, fluxes are higher in the C\(\oplus \) case that in the C\(\ominus \) one; on the contrary a green arrow indicate that higher fluxes are found in the C\(\ominus \) case. Black arrows belong to reactions whose Kolmogorov–Smirnov cumulative value is under the threshold of 9.

When comparing the average behavior of single flux values as a function of glucose uptake for the C\(\ominus \) and C\(\oplus \) cases, it is possible to notice (Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)) some results confirming that our approach is actually describing fluxes typical of the Crabtree effect and of the Crabtree-negative phenotype. Besides the most obvious differences relative to reactions ascribable to fermentation and respiration, other fluxes belonging to glycolysis (e.g. F16P \(\rightarrow \) Trp in Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)a) show higher values in the case of C\(\oplus \) solutions with respect to fluxes distributions classified under the C\(\ominus \) phenotype; suggesting, in keeping with experimental results (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)), that the glycolytic pathway is enhanced in the Crabtree phenotype. Besides glycolysis other fluxes show a behavior in accordance with the expected phenotype:

+ pyruvate \(\rightarrow \) acetylCoA (pyruvate carboxylase, Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)e) is enhanced in C\(\ominus \) phenotype (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)) as it is a bridge towards the TCA cycle and the respiratory metabolism of yeast;
+ pyruvate \(\rightarrow \) acetaldehyde (pyruvate decarboxylase, Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)g) is enhanced in C\(\oplus \) phenotype (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)) as it leads to ethanol production, final step of the fermentative metabolism;
+ acetaldehyde \(\rightarrow \) acetate (acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)f) and acetate \(\rightarrow \) acetylCoA (acetylCoA synthetase, Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)d), redirecting C towards the TCA cycle, have higher values in the case of C\(\ominus \) (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26); Urk et al. [1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38));
+ acetylCoA \(\rightarrow \) fatty acids (Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)c) and ribose 5-phosphate \(\rightarrow \) nucleic acids (Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)b) (Papini et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR26)): according to Urk et al. ([1990](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR38)) show higher fluxes in C\(\ominus \) cells because of a greater production of biomass in comparison to C\(\oplus \) cells (Hagman et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR15)).

Moreover the eeFBA approach has revealed also less expected differences in fluxes related to redox cofactors such as NAD\(^+\) and NADH (Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)h, i): indeed C\(\oplus \) solutions show a higher uptake of NADH and a higher outflow of NAD\(^+\) in comparison to C\(\ominus \) solutions, suggesting a greater use of NADH and a surplus of NAD\(^+\).

## 4 Conclusions
FBA has been used extensively in computational studies of metabolism both in microorganisms and more recently in mammalian cells. By way of example, FBA analysis of genome-scale models of the bacterium _Escherichia coli_, not only allowed model-driven discovery and prediction of phenotypic traits of the microorganism, but has also successfully been used to direct metabolic engineering of microbial cells towards maximized production of a biochemical compound of industrial use [reviewed in McCloskey et al. ([2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR21))].

On the contrary, the use of FBA to probe the design principles of metabolism, or the plasticity of a given metabolic network to evolving genetic or environmental conditions has received less attention (Schuetz et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR32); Harcombe et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR16)). Our method allows one to compare different metabolic phenotypes and we applied it to the study of Crabtree-positive and Crabtree-negative yeasts using a core model that summarizes the major features of yeast energetic metabolism. In our approach the structure of the network is maintained fixed, while systematic variation of the maximal glucose uptake rate simulates a change in the environment. Major input-output features of Crabtree-positive and Crabtree-negative yeasts are described by algebraic rules. By adopting an evolutionary algorithm, an extensive set of OFs was sampled and clearly distinct ensembles of solutions (i.e., specific distributions of metabolic fluxes), one in agreement with the definition of Crabtree-positive yeasts and the other in agreement with the definition of Crabtree-negative yeasts, have been obtained. Although evolutionary algorithms have been previously employed within the context of FBA, e.g. to rapidly identify gene deletion strategies to optimize a desired phenotypic objective function (Patil et al. [2005](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR27)) or for nonlinear optimization problems (Supudomchok et al. [2008](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR35)), to our knowledge, we are the first ones to use evolutionary algorithms in order to seek functional states that are in agreement with experimentally observed phenotypes.

The ensembles have been further characterized and refined by means of a cluster analysis that partitioned solutions into subsets, which may represent distinct sub-phenotypes for which the same metabolic dynamics holds (see Segre et al. [2005](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR33)). These sub-clusters are particularly apparent in the heat map reported in Fig. [4](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig4) (grey rectangles), indicating that non-trivial differences in the flux distribution are compatible with the same metabolic phenotype. Our method may thus be particularly well suited to identification and characterization of metabolic plasticity in all those cases where a given high-level phenotype, such as cancer, may be compatible with different metabolic configurations (Alberghina et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR1); Chiu et al. [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR8); Diaz-Ruiz et al. [2011](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR12); Soga [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR34)), or metabolic rearrangements originated from changing evolutionary, physio-pathological or developmental constraints.

It is worth noting that if phenotypic definitions are not too restrictive, the analysis of the typical behavior of the ensemble provides a dynamic description of the emergent behaviors of the network, actually providing insights on the design principles of the studied network. According to this perspective, although possible, we did not impose that our solutions must be performant in terms of biomass production because even poorly performing phenotypes can be instructive in the search for the “design principles” of the phenomenon under study. Moreover, a phenotype that is optimal under a given selective condition, may be less than optimal—and even dangerous—under a different condition, as it has been shown for energetic metabolism in mammals (Kitano et al. [2004](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR18)). Indeed, a gene may be beneficial, or even essential, only under specific circumstances or in a specific genetic background, for instance in combination with a “synthetic lethal gene”. Recent experimental and computational results indicate that two yeast strains differing in their ability to respond to changes in carbon source, exhibit the same fitness level in static environmental conditions (Razinkov et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR30)). Similarly, remarkable metabolic diversity has been observed in a single-resource chemostat despite the absence of ecological or periodic selection (Maharjan et al. [2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR19)), revealing the coexistence of subpopulations with diverse phenotypes on different parts of the same fitness landscape (Beardmore et al. [2011](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR5)). A dynamic alteration of the environment (i.e., a change in carbon source), may eventually significantly alter the fitness of coexisting strains (Razinkov et al. [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#ref-CR30)).

The results presented in this paper do not attempt to investigate in fine metabolic detail the molecular basis of the CE in yeast. Rather they are intended to provide the proof-of-principle of the method’s potential. The obtained results are in keeping with available information and may even provide testable hypotheses, such as the predicted major flux of glucose towards fatty acids in Crabtree-negative versus Crabtree-positive strains (Fig. [5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11047-014-9439-4#Fig5)c). Nevertheless, in depth analysis of the subject requires the use of a genome-scale model, where non-obvious interactions among metabolic reactions can be studied in an unbiased way.
